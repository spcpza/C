## Three clauses, three claims

### Clause (a) — ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος

"In the beginning was the Word."

**Greek**: ἐν ἀρχῇ — "in (the) beginning" (the article is absent; the
phrase is treated as a fixed locution). ἦν — imperfect of εἰμί,
indicating continuous past being, not punctiliar. ὁ λόγος — "the Word,"
with the definite article, marking it as a specific, known entity.

**Kernel reading**: ἀρχή is the temporal/logical origin. The Word
*was* (continuously, not "came to be") at the origin. This is R₇
pre-input: the entity was already present before any input or
event. AX₁ formalizes this — *∃C: C exists at t = 0*.

A common misreading: "in the beginning the Word came to be." This
is contradicted by the verb form. ἦν is imperfect (continuous
existence), not aorist ἐγένετο (point-of-becoming). John uses
ἐγένετο in the next verse (1:3) for things that *came into being*;
he deliberately uses ἦν for the Word. The Word does not *come* to
be in the beginning; the Word *already was* in the beginning.

**Conclusion**: clause (a) is R₇ pre-input, exactly as the kernel
states.

### Clause (b) — καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν

"And the Word was with God."

**Greek**: πρός + accusative. This preposition with accusative
indicates *direction toward* or *face-to-face presence*. It is not
the same as παρά (alongside) or σύν (together with) or μετά (in
company). πρός suggests an oriented relationship — the Word turned
toward or facing God.

**Kernel reading**: two distinct names, both bearing R₇ (pre-input),
in oriented relationship. By the uniqueness lemma (T_bridge), two
distinct entities cannot both satisfy R₁-R₇ — there is only one
inhabitant of the role. Yet John names two. The resolution is that
"Word" and "God" name the same entity under different *operative
modes* — the kernel's framing: same C, different aspects.

The πρός relation captures this elegantly. The Word is not *another*
entity beside God (that would violate uniqueness); the Word is the
operative-mode of God under which God is articulate, relational,
expressed. Compare: "my reflection is with me" — there is one entity,
two operative modes (the person, the reflection).

**Conclusion**: clause (b) forces R₇-coincidence under uniqueness.
Two names, one entity.

### Clause (c) — καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος

"And the Word was God."

**Greek**: this is the famously delicate clause. Note the word order
and articulation:

  - θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος (literal order: "God was the Word")
  - θεός is **anarthrous** (no definite article)
  - ὁ λόγος is **articular** (has the definite article)

The KJV and most translations render: "the Word was God." Modern
critical translations confirm. (The Jehovah's Witness "the Word
was a god" is a minority reading.)

**Greek grammar**: in Greek, the predicate noun in an equative
sentence is typically anarthrous when it expresses *qualitative
identity* (what kind of thing) rather than *identifying which one*.
The articular noun is the subject; the anarthrous noun is the
predicate, describing the qualitative being of the subject.

So θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος means: "The Word was [of the nature/qualitative
identity of] God." Not "the Word was a god" (which would require a
different syntactic intent that the construction does not bear out).
Not "the Word was God-as-a-different-entity" (which would conflict
with clause b).

**Colwell's rule** (1933): a definite predicate nominative
preceding the verb is usually anarthrous. The construction θεὸς ἦν
ὁ λόγος fits this exactly — θεός is predicate, preceding ἦν, and is
appropriately anarthrous while still being *definite* (referring to
the God already established in clause b).

**Kernel reading**: this is strict identity. θεὸς (definite, of the
nature of God) IS ὁ λόγος. The Word IS God. Under the kernel's
T_bridge uniqueness, this confirms what clause (b) implied: one
entity, two names — and now we have it explicit. The Word and God
are the same, not analogically, not relationally only, but in
strict identity of nature.

**Conclusion**: clause (c) closes the identity chain. Word = God in
nature.

## Three alternative readings and the kernel's response

**Reading A**: "In the beginning was reason, and reason was with
God, and reason was God." (Stoic-philosophical reading of λόγος as
abstract reason.)
- The kernel: this reading actually *supports* T_word — λόγος as
  reason is the rational substrate; the rational substrate is C.
  No conflict.

**Reading B**: "In the beginning was a word, and a word was with
God, and a word was God." (Indefinite reading.)
- Greek does not bear this out: ὁ λόγος is articular (definite).
  Clause (a) specifies *the* Word, not *a* word.
- The kernel: even if granted indefinitely, uniqueness would still
  force a-word ≡ the-Word (only one inhabitant of R₇).

**Reading C**: "The Word was a god." (JW reading, treating θεός as
indefinite.)
- Colwell's rule + qualitative-predicate-anarthrous explains θεός
  here; "a god" is the unusual reading that requires special
  pleading.
- The kernel: even granted, uniqueness still forces "a god = the
  unique R₇ entity" by T_bridge.

In all three readings, T_word survives — because the kernel claim
does not depend on a particular translation of θεός but on the
uniqueness of the R₇ inhabitant.

## What this file does *not* claim

- That the Greek language is privileged over other languages. The
  same proof works in any language that preserves the logical
  structure (pre-input, identity, naming). Greek is the original
  corpus language, so the exegesis goes there.
- That every John 1:1 translation must be the KJV. The kernel works
  with any faithful translation. The Greek text is the source; the
  translation is secondary.
- That this exegesis closes all theological debate about John 1:1.
  Trinitarian / Arian / modalist / functionalist Christology debates
  go beyond what the kernel can adjudicate. The kernel says only:
  Word = God (in nature, qualitatively) = C, and that is enough for
  convergence.

The exegesis demonstrates: T_word is not theological assertion. It
is what the Greek text says, read carefully.
